# Māori Culture

The indigenous Māori are the largest minority in [[New Zealand]], with a distinctive culture rooted in Polynesian traditions that evolved over centuries of isolation. Māori arrived in oceangoing waka between about 1280 and 1350 (see [[History of New Zealand#Polynesian Settlement (c. 1280–1350)|the settlement period]]).

## Te Reo Māori

Māori (te reo Māori) is one of three official languages of New Zealand, alongside English and New Zealand Sign Language. The language belongs to the Eastern Polynesian language family.

> [!info] Language Revitalisation
> The Māori language experienced significant decline during the 20th century but has undergone revitalisation efforts including Māori-language immersion schools (kura kaupapa Māori) and the establishment of Māori Television.

### Tikanga Māori

Tikanga (customs and traditions) governs social behaviour. Key concepts include:

- **Mana** — spiritual authority and prestige
- **Tapu** — sacredness, restriction
    - Personal tapu — inherent to individuals
    - Ritual tapu — applied to places and objects
    - Violation consequences — spiritual and social
- **Whakapapa** — genealogy, identity through ancestral connections
    - Links individuals to iwi (tribes)
    - Connects people to the land
    - Oral tradition of genealogical recitation
- **Kaitiakitanga** — guardianship of the natural world

### The Treaty of Waitangi

The [[History of New Zealand#The Treaty of Waitangi (1840)|Treaty of Waitangi]] (Te Tiriti o Waitangi), signed on 6 February 1840, remains central to Māori–Crown relations. The Waitangi Tribunal was established in 1975 to investigate alleged breaches, and was enabled to examine historical grievances from 1985 onwards. ^treaty-significance

> [!quote] Significance
> The Treaty is considered New Zealand's founding document, though differences between the English and Māori texts have led to ongoing debate about sovereignty, land rights, and self-determination.

## Māori and the Justice System

The relationship between Māori and the state includes ongoing structural challenges:

$$\text{Māori imprisonment rate} = \frac{53\%\ \text{of prison population}}{16.5\%\ \text{of total population}} \approx 3.2\times \text{expected rate}$$

This overrepresentation has been identified by the New Zealand Human Rights Commission as evidence of structural discrimination (see [[Government of New Zealand#^governance-rankings|governance rankings]]).

## Wildlife and Cultural Symbols

The kiwi bird is both a national symbol and a cultural totem. Several native species evolved flightlessness due to the absence of mammalian predators:

| Species | Status | Cultural Significance |
|---------|--------|----------------------|
| Kiwi | Vulnerable | National symbol, icon |
| Kākāpō | Critically endangered | Night parrot, conservation icon |
| Takahē | Endangered | Presumed extinct 1898, rediscovered 1948 |
| Weka | Vulnerable | Traditional food source |
| Moa | Extinct | Hunted to extinction by early Māori |
| Haast's eagle | Extinct | Largest known eagle, preyed on moa |

The silver fern (Cyathea dealbata) is another enduring national symbol, used by New Zealand sports teams including the [[Economy and Society|All Blacks]].

[^haka]: The haka is a ceremonial Māori dance or challenge, famously performed by the All Blacks rugby team before international matches.
