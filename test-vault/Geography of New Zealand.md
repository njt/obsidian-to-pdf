# Geography of New Zealand

![[nz-map.png]]

[[New Zealand]] is long and narrow — over 1,600 km along its north-north-east axis with a maximum width of 400 km — with about 15,000 km of coastline.

## The Two Main Islands

The **North Island** (Te Ika-a-Māui) is marked by volcanism. The highly active Taupō Volcanic Zone has formed a large volcanic plateau, punctuated by the North Island's highest mountain, Mount Ruapehu at 2,797 m.

The **South Island** (Te Waipounamu) is divided along its length by the Southern Alps (Kā Tiritiri o te Moana). There are 18 peaks over 3,000 m, the highest being Aoraki / Mount Cook at 3,724 m.

> [!warning] Earthquake Risk
> New Zealand straddles the dynamic boundary between the Pacific and Indo-Australian Plates. The country is prone to earthquakes, with the Alpine Fault, Puysegur Trench, and Hikurangi Trough all contributing to seismic activity.

The Southern Alps were formed by compression of the crust beside the Alpine Fault. New Zealand is part of Zealandia, a microcontinent nearly half the size of Australia that gradually submerged after breaking away from the Gondwanan supercontinent about $25$ million years ago.

## Climate

New Zealand's climate is predominantly temperate maritime (Köppen: Cfb), with mean annual temperatures ranging from $10\,°\text{C}$ in the south to $16\,°\text{C}$ in the north.

- **Extremes**
    - Historical maximum: $42.4\,°\text{C}$ in Rangiora, Canterbury
    - Historical minimum: $-25.6\,°\text{C}$ in Ranfurly, Otago
- **Rainfall**
    - Christchurch (driest major city): 618 mm/year
    - Wellington (wettest major city): ~1,200 mm/year
- **Sunshine**
    - Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch: >2,000 hours/year
    - Northern/northeastern South Island: 2,400–2,500 hours/year

The general snow season runs from early June to early October.

## Biodiversity

About $82\%$ of New Zealand's indigenous vascular plants are endemic, covering 1,944 species across 65 genera. An estimated $40\%$ of the country's ~2,300 lichen-forming fungi species are also endemic.

> [!tip] Unique Wildlife
> More penguin species are found in New Zealand than in any other country, with 13 of the world's 18 penguin species present. The country's forests were dominated by birds, and the lack of mammalian predators led species like the [[Māori Culture|kiwi]], kākāpō, weka, and takahē to evolve flightlessness.

Before humans arrived, an estimated $80\%$ of the land was covered in forest. Since human arrival, almost half of the country's vertebrate species have become extinct, including at least 51 birds, three frogs, three lizards, one freshwater fish, and one bat.[^deforestation]

[^deforestation]: Massive deforestation occurred after Polynesian settlement, with around half the forest cover lost to fire. By 1997, forest occupied only 23% of the land.
